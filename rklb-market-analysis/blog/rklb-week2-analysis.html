Rocket Lab USA: Marketing Probability and Bayesian Analysis

This five-part project applies promotional strategy, customer segmentation, conditional probability, fair random selection, and Bayesian updating to a projected Rocket Lab USA marketing campaign.

POST 01

Promotional Event

Rocket Lab USA should launch a projected Mission Ready Challenge, a business-to-business campaign aimed at organizations planning small-satellite, research, defense, and public-sector missions. Qualified participants would register for a technical webinar, submit a short mission concept, and receive a launch-readiness assessment. The event would introduce potential customers to Electron, HASTE, Photon, and Rocket Lab’s broader Space Systems portfolio (Rocket Lab USA, 2026).

The campaign begins with 1,200 targeted contacts and projects 400 registrations, 60 qualified leads, 12 proposal requests, and two expected contracts. This structure allows the campaign to function as both an educational event and a measurable sales-development program.

Campaign cost$185,000
Expected contribution$900,000
Projected ROI386.5%
Projected ROI = ($900,000 − $185,000) ÷ $185,000 = 3.865, or 386.5%

Projected values are academic assumptions and are not Rocket Lab guidance.

POST 02

Target Customers

The target pool contains four customer groups: university research laboratories, satellite startups, established aerospace and defense firms, and public-sector mission teams. Research laboratories would be reached through scientific conferences and partnerships. Startups would be reached through incubators, venture networks, and targeted digital media. Aerospace and defense firms require account-based marketing, while public-sector teams require procurement briefings and technical outreach.

POST 03

Selected Sampling

Within the projected sample of 400 customers, 145 are women. The probability of randomly selecting a woman from the marketing sample is therefore:

P(W | Sample) = 145 ÷ 400 = 0.3625 = 36.25%

The broader potential-customer pool contains 510 women among 1,200 people:

P(W | Potential) = 510 ÷ 1,200 = 0.4250 = 42.50%

The 6.25 percentage-point difference does not independently prove discrimination. It may reflect occupational demographics, seniority requirements, list-selection methods, conference attendance, regional coverage, or nonresponse. However, the difference is large enough to justify reviewing prospect sources, category-level selection rates, and inclusion criteria.

POST 04

Lottery Analysis

If one person is randomly selected from the 400-person sample for a Hawaii trip, the chance of selection from each category is based on that category’s number of eligible entries.

  • University research laboratories: 120 ÷ 400 = 30.00%
  • Satellite startups: 110 ÷ 400 = 27.50%
  • Aerospace and defense: 95 ÷ 400 = 23.75%
  • Public-sector mission teams: 75 ÷ 400 = 18.75%

A fair drawing should provide one unique entry to every eligible participant, publish the eligibility rules in advance, remove duplicates consistently, and use a reproducible random-number process. The seed, eligible-entry file, and final output should be preserved for audit. If the award is based on merit rather than chance, multiple reviewers should use a standardized scoring rubric that excludes protected characteristics.

POST 05

Bayesian Analysis

A Bayesian model can update the campaign’s expected conversion rate as new evidence becomes available. The analysis begins with a Beta(3, 97) prior distribution, representing an initial expected conversion rate of 3.00%. A pilot campaign then produces eight conversions from 120 qualified leads.

Posterior = Beta(3 + 8, 97 + 112) = Beta(11, 209)
Posterior mean = 11 ÷ (11 + 209) = 0.0500 = 5.00%

The 95% credible interval is approximately 2.55% to 8.25%. The posterior probability that the conversion rate exceeds 4% is approximately 72.8%. Rocket Lab should set a formal decision rule, such as expanding the campaign only when the posterior probability of exceeding the break-even rate reaches 80%.

The model should also be updated separately by customer category, message, and acquisition channel. This approach preserves prior knowledge, incorporates new evidence, quantifies uncertainty, and directs spending toward the segments most likely to produce profitable conversion (Gelman et al., 2013).
